Water Filtration in Black Granite Mining

A Sustainable Approach to Water Management

1. Introduction

Granite mining, essential for construction and infrastructure, presents significant challenges, especially concerning water usage. This project addresses the critical issue of water wastage and transportation costs associated with the vast amounts of water used in granite cutting processes. By implementing a comprehensive filtration system, the aim is to minimize water wastage, reduce operational costs, and contribute to sustainable mining practices.

The core idea behind this project was to adopt coagulation followed by adsorption through natural adsorbents, a method successfully used in the granite industry for wastewater treatment. This approach increases the settling speed for suspended solids and allows for the return of water to the production line without turbidity. The objective is to remove colloidal material in wastewater so that suspended solids settle by gravity.

1.1 Background and Objectives

Granite mining operations consume substantial quantities of water, with cutting machines alone utilizing up to 1000 liters per hour. This demand often necessitates costly water transportation from distant sources. To mitigate these challenges, this project focuses on developing an efficient water filtration system utilizing locally available water sources, including groundwater seepage from deeper mining areas.

1.2 Scope and Context

The primary objective is to design and implement a multi-stage filtration process to treat water extracted from granite mining operations. This process consists of four main stages: primary settling, mesh filtration, chemical treatment, and chlorine treatment. Each stage targets specific impurities like suspended solids, dissolved solids, and turbidity, to produce clean water suitable for industrial use and potentially drinking water with additional chlorination.

1.3 Motivation

The motivation for this project stems from the urgent need to address the environmental and economic challenges of water usage in granite mining. By optimizing water management through filtration, the goal is to minimize wastage, reduce transportation costs, and alleviate the environmental impact of mining operations. Utilizing local water sources and cost-effective filtration methods enhances sustainability and efficiency.

2. Literature Review

Previous studies have explored physicochemical treatments for marble and stone cutting wastewater. Arslan Eletal. conducted a study in Turkey titled "physicochemical treatment of marble wastewater and recycling of sludge," investigating coagulation and flocculation processes and parameters like turbidity, total solids (TS), suspended solids (SS), and chemical oxygen demand (COD). Nasserdine Ketal. in Hebron also focused on improving effluent quality from stone industries using coagulation processes, employing the Jar Test for optimization and treatment possibility studies.

In such physicochemical treatments, a large amount of sludge can be created, which requires further removal. Marble factories, for instance, use significant water for cutting, washing, and polishing, with cutting alone producing approximately 20-30% of the block's weight as fine particles.

3. Effects of High Turbidity Water

High turbidity water can have several significant effects, including environmental, health, and economic impacts.

3.1 Environmental Effects:

  • Reduced Light Penetration: High turbidity reduces sunlight penetration, inhibiting photosynthesis and limiting aquatic plant growth, which affects overall productivity and biodiversity of aquatic habitats.
  • Sedimentation: Suspended particles settle and accumulate, leading to excessive sedimentation that can smother benthic habitats, disrupt aquatic ecosystems, and degrade water quality.
  • Habitat Alteration: High turbidity can alter the physical structure and composition of aquatic habitats, impacting the distribution and abundance of aquatic species and reducing habitat suitability for sensitive organisms.

3.2 Health Effects:

  • Microbial Contamination: Turbid water may harbor higher concentrations of bacteria, viruses, and other pathogens, increasing the risk of waterborne diseases.
  • Chemical Contaminants: Suspended particles can adsorb and transport chemical contaminants like heavy metals, pesticides, and organic pollutants, posing health risks through ingestion, inhalation, or dermal exposure.

3.3 Economic Effects:

  • Impact on Fisheries: High turbidity can impair fish habitat and reduce food resources, leading to declines in fish populations and significant economic implications for fishing industries.
  • Water Treatment Costs: Turbid water requires more extensive treatment processes, leading to higher treatment costs that burden water utilities and consumers.
  • Loss of Recreational Value: Turbid water detracts from the aesthetic appeal and recreational value of water bodies, negatively impacting tourism and recreation-based businesses.

Overall, high turbidity water can have far-reaching consequences for ecosystems, public health, and economic well-being. Effective management strategies are essential to mitigate these adverse effects and ensure water resource sustainability.

4. Methodology

The "Water Filtration in Black Granite Mining" project involves a systematic approach to design, implement, and optimize a multi-stage filtration process tailored to the specific requirements and challenges of granite mining operations. This methodology encompasses various steps, including water extraction, primary settling, mesh filtration, chemical treatment, and chlorine treatment, each aimed at efficiently removing impurities to produce clean, usable water.

4.2.1 Water Extraction

The first step involves extracting water from the granite mining site using 10HP pumps. This water, containing a high concentration of solids and impurities, is transported to designated storage tanks for further treatment. Groundwater seepage from deeper mining areas may also contribute to the water source.

4.2.2 Primary Settling

After extraction, the water undergoes primary settling in open wells for 38 to 48 hours, allowing heavy solids to settle by gravity. This enhances the efficiency of subsequent filtration stages by removing a significant portion of solids upfront.

4.2.3 Mesh Filtration

Following primary settling, the water is transferred to filter bags for mesh filtration. Four filter bags, each with a pore size of approximately 130 microns, are used to capture solid materials, including diamond dust and other fine particles. This stage is crucial for further purifying the water by removing smaller particles that may have escaped primary settling. Secondary settling, lasting 48 hours, accompanies mesh filtration to allow any remaining suspended solids to settle.

4.2.4 Chemical Treatment

Upon completion of mesh filtration, ferric chloride is added to the water in storage tanks at a specified dosage to facilitate impurity precipitation. This initiates a coagulation process, where suspended solids aggregate and form larger flocs that settle at the bottom of the tanks. The dosage is determined by jar tests on the extracted water. After ferric chloride addition, the water settles for one day, significantly improving water quality.

4.2.5 Chlorine Treatment (Optional)

The final stage involves chlorine treatment to ensure the microbiological safety of the filtered water. Chlorine is added at a prescribed dosage, typically around 1 gram per 1000 liters, as recommended by municipal guidelines for drinking water. This disinfects the water, eliminating pathogens and making it suitable for various applications, including industrial use and potentially drinking water with additional treatment.

4.3 Jar Test Details and Calculations

The jar test is a critical component, providing insights into treatment requirements and optimal dosages of chemicals like ferric chloride.

4.3.1 Jar Test Procedure:

The jar test analyzed a sample of extracted water to determine its turbidity and treatment requirements. Measured parameters included:

  • Temperature: $20^{\circ}C$
  • pH value: 7.2
  • Turbidity: 280 NTU
  • Electric conductivity: $3400~\mu S/cm$
  • Total solids: $3600~mg/L$
  • Total suspended solids: $1600~mg/L$
  • Total dissolved solids: $2000~mg/L$

4.3.3 Ferric Chloride Dosage Calculation:

The jar test indicated that 0.02 grams of ferric chloride was used to convert 28 NTU turbidity water to 0.9 NTU. By calculation, around 800 grams of ferric chloride is used to convert 1000 liters of water with 250-280 NTU to 0.8-1 NTU.

4.4 Mechanism of Ferric Chloride (FeCL3)

Ferric chloride in turbidity water involves coagulation and flocculation.

  • Coagulation: Ferric chloride dissociates into ferric ions ($Fe^{3+}$) and chloride ions ($Cl^-$). The ferric ions act as coagulants, destabilizing suspended particles through charge neutralization and adsorption. Negatively charged particles are attracted to the positively charged ferric ions.
  • Formation of Floc: As particles contact ferric ions, they coagulate, forming larger, denser particles known as floc.
  • Flocculation: Gentle mixing (flocculation) causes floc particles to collide and adhere, forming larger, denser aggregates.
  • Settling and Filtration: The flocculated particles settle by gravity (sedimentation). The settled floc forms sludge, leaving clarified water. This water is then separated via decantation, sedimentation, or filtration, reducing turbidity and improving quality.

In summary, ferric chloride acts as a coagulant by neutralizing charges on suspended particles, promoting their aggregation into larger floc particles through coagulation and flocculation mechanisms. This process facilitates the removal of suspended solids and turbidity from water, ultimately improving its clarity and quality.

The project overview summarized key components: water extraction, settling, filtration, and treatment stages, emphasizing benefits like improved water quality and enhanced operational efficiency.

5.8. Equipment and Materials:

Successful implementation relies on various equipment and materials. Key items include:

  • 10HP pumps: Used for water extraction from mining sites and groundwater sources, providing necessary flow rate and pressure.
  • Storage tanks: Utilized for primary settling, chemical treatment, and chlorine disinfection, providing adequate capacity for various processes.
  • Filter bags: With a pore size of approximately 130 microns, employed for mesh filtration, capturing fine particles and contaminants.
  • Ferric chloride: Used as a coagulant in the chemical treatment stage to facilitate impurity precipitation and improve water quality.
  • Chlorine: Utilized as a disinfectant in the final stage to ensure microbiological safety of filtered water.
  • Monitoring devices: Turbidity meters, pH meters, conductivity meters, and chlorine analyzers to assess water quality and treatment performance.

Piping, valves, instrumentation, and safety equipment are also essential components.

A comprehensive cost analysis assessed the financial implications. This included:

  • Capital investment: Expenses for equipment, construction, installation, and infrastructure upgrades.
  • Operational costs: Recurring expenses for system operation, maintenance, repair, and consumables.
  • Consumables: Costs for ferric chloride and chlorine.
  • Labor costs: Wages, salaries, benefits, and training expenses.
  • Energy costs: Electricity consumption for pumps and equipment, and potential savings from efficiency.
  • Regulatory compliance costs: Permits, licenses, and reporting.

This analysis helped assess financial feasibility, identify cost-saving opportunities, and optimize resource allocation.

6. Results

The successful implementation of the water filtration project yielded significant improvements in water quality, environmental sustainability, and cost efficiency.

6.1 Chemical Characteristics of Water After Filtration:

  • Turbidity: 0.92 NTU - 1 NTU (a substantial reduction from initial 280 NTU)

6.2 Cost Savings:

During the two-month implementation, significant cost savings were realized.

  • Approximately 9 lakh liters of water were filtered, reducing costly water transportation and wastage.
  • The use of 800 kgs of ferric chloride resulted in substantial cost savings compared to traditional methods.
  • Estimated savings of 3 to 4 lakh rupees in operational costs for one cutting machine alone.

6.3 Project Result Summary:

The filtration system enhanced water clarity and purity, making it suitable for various industrial applications. Tangible cost savings were achieved through reduced water wastage and operational expenses. These savings contribute to the profitability and sustainability of granite mining operations while minimizing environmental impact. In conclusion, the project achieved its objectives of improving water quality, reducing operational costs, and promoting environmental stewardship.

7. Conclusion

The implementation of the water filtration project in black granite mining operations marks a significant milestone in pursuing sustainability, efficiency, and environmental stewardship. Through meticulous planning, innovative design, and dedicated execution, the project achieved its objectives of enhancing water quality, reducing operational costs, and promoting responsible resource management.

The project's success is evident in tangible improvements in water characteristics after filtration, with significant reductions in turbidity levels and enhancements in overall water chemistry. These improvements benefit mining operations, preserve aquatic ecosystems, and protect public health.

Moreover, the project demonstrated substantial cost savings through efficient water treatment methods and optimized resource utilization. Minimizing water wastage, reducing costly transportation, and implementing effective filtration techniques generated significant financial returns while minimizing environmental impact.

Total Sustainable Waste Management

An Internship Report on Integrated Solid Waste Management at EPTRI

1. Abstract & Introduction

This report details the processes observed at Hyderabad Integrated Solid Waste Management (HISWM), a facility that treats 7,000 to 9,000 tons of municipal solid waste daily. The internship focused on understanding sustainable treatment processes for solid waste, including the conversion of plastic to oil, generation of energy from waste, management of landfills, and production of Compressed Bio-Gas (CBG). The primary goal of these integrated systems is to reuse and reprocess waste, turning it into valuable products while minimizing environmental harm from issues like plastic pollution and landfill leachate.

2. Waste Processing at HISWM

Upon arrival, waste undergoes a systematic segregation process. It is fed into trommels for separation. Smaller organic particles are sent to a windrow platform for aerobic composting, where they are turned into nutrient-rich compost over a month. Larger particles and non-compostable materials are sent to the Refuse Derived Fuel (RDF) bunker. This RDF is either landfilled or used in the waste-to-energy plant.

Leachate Management

A critical aspect of the facility is managing leachate, the contaminated liquid that drains from landfills. HISWM has implemented systems to control leachate flow, including solar evaporation ponds and a dedicated Leachate Treatment Plant that uses Reverse Osmosis and UV treatment to convert it into a usable form of water.

3. Waste to Energy Plant

A major component of HISWM is the waste-to-energy plant, where RDF is used to generate electricity. The waste goes through three stages before combustion: stocking (2 days), fermenting (shuffled to dry), and feeding.

Mass Burning Process:

  • A crane feeds the dried waste into a hopper, which leads to the furnace. About 24-30 tons of waste are burned per hour.
  • **Dry Zone:** Remaining moisture is evaporated.
  • **Combustion Zone:** Waste is burned at 1000°C, turning most of it into ash.
  • **Burn-out Zone:** Ensures complete combustion.

The intense heat boils water in a shell boiler system, creating high-pressure steam (400-450°C). This steam drives a turbine at 6,200 RPM to generate up to 24 MW of power. The used steam is then condensed in cooling towers and reused, following the Rankine cycle. Flue gases from the furnace are treated with lime and activated carbon to remove toxins before being released.

4. Landfill Management & Scientific Capping

Landfills are a major source of greenhouse gases like methane. To mitigate this, HISWM uses a scientific "capping" process for its landfills, which prevents environmental contamination and allows for resource recovery.

The 7 Layers of Landfill Capping:

  1. Sand: Provides a stable base over the RDF.
  2. Black Soil: Rich in lime, which helps reduce the evolution of methane and CO2.
  3. Geomembrane Liner: A black plastic sheet that provides a stiff, impermeable barrier.
  4. Filter Fabric: A white duster-like material that absorbs and filters toxic liquids.
  5. Pebbles/Gravel: A drainage layer.
  6. Red Sand: A medium for plantation.
  7. Green Grass Belt: Prevents soil erosion and provides a green cover.

This system effectively stops water and air pollution, allows for the controlled extraction of methane for CBG production, and prevents leachate from contaminating groundwater.

5. Compressed Bio-Gas (CBG) Production

Methane gas from the capped landfills is captured and converted into valuable Compressed Bio-Gas. The raw gas (45-50% methane) is purified to over 90% methane concentration.

Purification Process:

  • Gas is collected and pumped into an NaOH scrubber to remove acidic gases like H2S.
  • A water scrubber then removes excess moisture.
  • The gas passes through five MPSE towers for cooling, drying, and removal of CO2 and N2.
  • A final patented unit removes oxygen to prevent combustion hazards.

The purified methane is condensed, stored in a 2-ton surge vessel, and then compressed to fill cylinders for transport to gas stations.

6. Plastic to Oil Conversion (Pyrolysis)

The report also covers a project on converting plastic waste into oil using pyrolysis. Plastic, being a polymer of long hydrocarbon chains, can be broken down by heat in the absence of oxygen.

Process Overview:

Plastic is heated in a closed furnace. The heat breaks the long polymer chains into smaller molecules, forming a gas. This gas is then passed through a condenser, where it cools and turns into a liquid—a combustible oil. The key is to prevent combustion by limiting oxygen, which differentiates pyrolysis from simple burning. The non-condensable gases can be used to fuel the process itself, making it more sustainable.

7. Conclusion

The integrated approach at HISWM demonstrates that solid waste is not merely refuse but a resource. Through a combination of waste-to-energy incineration, scientific landfilling with gas capture, CBG production, and pyrolysis, it is possible to treat municipal waste in an environmentally sound and economically viable manner. These projects effectively turn potential hazards into assets, contributing to a circular economy and a more sustainable urban future.
